# Opening Moves — Expanded

Load this when drafting a piece >500 words or when you're stuck on
the first paragraph.

## The four scaffolds

### 1. "You just did the obvious thing" — setup and deflate

The reader walks in, does the thing they'd normally do, and it
under-delivers. You acknowledge the moment, then pivot.

**Template:**
> You sit down to [X]. You do [obvious thing]. It comes back.
> You [mild reaction of satisfaction]. It hasn't [delivered what
> they actually need].

**Why it works:** Establishes rapport by showing you know the
reader's first instinct. Lets you pivot without calling them naive.

**Worked example (Terminal Forensics essay):**
> You type `ls`. It comes back. `Documents`. `Downloads`. Maybe
> `scripts/` if you're feeling fancy. You nod, as if the filesystem
> has just confirmed something meaningful. It hasn't.

### 2. Quoted rhetorical question

Start with a question phrased as the reader's inner thought, in
quotes. Immediately answer or redirect.

**Template:**
> "[Question the reader has been asking themselves]" you wonder,
> as [the situation that keeps reminding them of the question]
> happens again.

**Why it works:** Drops the reader into a named frustration instead
of making them wait for the article to earn their interest.

**Worked example (PyTorch piece):**
> "How do I initialize my layer dimensions in PyTorch without
> getting yelled at?"

### 3. Problem-shared

Name a frustration the reader has had. Don't propose a solution yet.

**Template:**
> [Common task] is the kind of problem that sounds like it
> shouldn't be a problem. And yet, here we are.

**Why it works:** Creates solidarity. The reader feels seen before
they feel instructed.

### 4. Setup-and-pivot

State the default approach as a fact. Immediately reject it.

**Template:**
> The instinct is to [X]. Don't. [X] [fails in specific way]. Here's
> what to do instead.

**Why it works:** Punches above its weight because the pivot is so
fast. Don't let the rejection become a long essay — make it one or
two sentences and move on.

---

## Combining scaffolds

The strongest openers often blend two. Example (Terminal Forensics):

1. Sets the scene (you just SSH'd somewhere unfamiliar).
2. Quotes the inner thought ("I know this," you say to yourself).
3. Does the obvious thing (types `ls`).
4. Deflates (it tells you nothing useful).
5. Promises the pivot (here's what to do instead).

That's scaffolds 1 + 2 merged, with scaffold 4 as the exit into the
body.

---

## Things to avoid in openers

- **Definitions.** "X is a thing that does Y." Cold and textbook.
- **Throat-clearing.** "In this article, we'll explore…" Cut it.
- **Over-promising.** "The ultimate guide to everything about X."
  Sets the reader up to be disappointed.
- **Under-promising.** "A quick note on X." The reader decides to
  read later. Later is never.
- **Rhetorical questions that obviously answer themselves.** "Have
  you ever wished you could code faster? Of course you have."
  Insulting.
