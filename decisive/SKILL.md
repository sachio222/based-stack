# Decisive

Framework for making better choices by counteracting four cognitive villains that corrupt every stage of the natural decision process. Based on the Heath brothers' central finding: **process matters more than analysis by a factor of six** (Lovallo/Sibony study of 1,048 business decisions). The WRAP framework provides one countermeasure per villain.

## Core Principle

**The quality of the decision process determines the quality of the outcome -- not the brilliance of the analysis.**

**The foundation:** Standard decision-making follows four steps: encounter a choice, analyze options, make a choice, live with it. Each step is corrupted by a specific cognitive villain: narrow framing, confirmation bias, short-term emotion, and overconfidence. The WRAP process wraps around your natural process to protect against each villain. "Whether or not" decisions fail 52% of the time; decisions with two or more alternatives fail only 32%. Multi-alternative decisions produce 6x more "very good" outcomes (40% vs. 6%).

## Scoring

**Goal: 10/10.** When reviewing or designing a decision process, rate it 0-10 based on adherence to the four WRAP steps below. A 10/10 means options are genuinely widened, assumptions are reality-tested with outside-view data, distance is achieved before deciding, and failure/success scenarios are pre-planned; lower scores indicate vulnerability to one or more villains. Always provide the current score and specific improvements needed to reach 10/10.

## The WRAP Framework

Each step counters one villain of decision-making.

| Step | Villain It Counters | Core Question |
|------|-------------------|---------------|
| **W -- Widen Your Options** | Narrow Framing | How can I expand my choice set? |
| **R -- Reality-Test Your Assumptions** | Confirmation Bias | How can I collect trustworthy, disconfirming information? |
| **A -- Attain Distance Before Deciding** | Short-Term Emotion | How can I overcome visceral feelings for my long-term values? |
| **P -- Prepare to Be Wrong** | Overconfidence | How can I plan for an uncertain future? |

### 1. Widen Your Options (W)

**Core concept:** Most decisions are too narrow by default. "Whether or not" framing reduces complex choices to a single binary. The spotlight effect -- attending to what's visible and neglecting everything outside it -- causes us to miss better alternatives. The goal is to generate more and better options before analyzing.

**Why it works:** Paul Nutt's 30-year study: only 29% of organizations considered more than one alternative. Multi-alternative decisions produced 6x more "very good" outcomes. Multitracking (considering options simultaneously) doesn't slow decisions -- Eisenhardt's Silicon Valley research found it actually speeds them up because leaders understand the landscape and have fallback plans.

**Key insights:**
- **Vanishing Options Test** -- "You cannot choose any of your current options. What else could you do?" Forces the spotlight to move.
- **AND Not OR** -- "Is there a way I can do this AND that?" Rejects binary framing.
- **Multitracking** -- develop multiple alternatives simultaneously. Benefits: better landscape understanding, undercuts politics, built-in fallback. Banner-ad study: simultaneous designers produced superior work and felt less criticized.
- **Opportunity Cost Prompt** -- "What am I giving up by choosing this?" Reminder of alternatives increased pass rates from 25% to 45%.
- **Bright Spots** -- look internally for what's already working. Kaiser Permanente: one hospital had already solved the sepsis problem; scaling it reduced mortality 28% below the national average.
- **Laddering Up** -- extract the crucial features of your problem and search for analogies in distant domains. Speedo's Fairhurst: redefined "faster swimsuit" as "anything that moves fast" -- found that sharkskin roughness reduces drag. 83% of swimming medals at 2000 Sydney Olympics.
- **Playlist** -- a pre-built list of stimuli questions for recurring decision types. Not a checklist (stops errors) -- a playlist (opens possibilities).

**Product applications:**

| Context | Application | Example |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| **Binary decision** | Apply AND Not OR | "Should we hire or not?" becomes "Could we hire part-time AND contract?" |
| **Stuck brainstorming** | Run Vanishing Options Test | Remove all current ideas; what would you try if none of them existed? |
| **Strategy planning** | Multitrack 2-3 approaches | Run parallel experiments rather than betting on one direction |
| **Budget cuts** | Use bright spots | Find the department already doing more with less; scale their approach |
| **Innovation** | Ladder up for distant analogies | Redefine the problem at a higher abstraction; who else has solved it? |
| **Recurring decisions** | Build a playlist | Pre-built questions that force broader thinking each time |

**Copy patterns:**
- "What else could you do if none of these options existed?"
- "Is there a way to do this AND that?"
- "What are you giving up by choosing this?"
- "Who has already solved this problem?"
- "Fall in love at least twice before deciding."

**Ethical boundary:** Multitracking should produce real alternatives, not sham options. If the team easily agrees on which option is best, you may have sham options. Genuine disagreement is a sign of real choice.

See: [references/widen-options.md](references/widen-options.md) for the full Vanishing Options Test, multitracking implementation, bright spots methodology, and laddering exercises.

### 2. Reality-Test Your Assumptions (R)

**Core concept:** Confirmation bias makes us seek information that confirms what we already believe. Even rigorous-looking data collection can be biased. The antidote: actively design ways to collect trustworthy, disconfirming information -- and separate the inside view (your optimistic assessment) from the outside view (base rates of how things like this usually go).

**Why it works:** Meta-analysis of 91 studies (8,000+ participants): people are more than twice as likely to favor confirming over disconfirming information. Tetlock's 20-year study: 284 expert forecasters making 82,361 predictions performed worse than simple base-rate extrapolation. Experts are reliable on base rates but unreliable on predictions.

**Key insights:**
- **Consider the Opposite** -- deliberately seek disconfirming arguments and evidence.
- **"What Would Have to Be True?"** (Roger Martin) -- shifts adversaries to collaborators. "What would have to be true for this option to be the very best choice?" Resolved 3-hour deadlock at Inmet Mining.
- **Disconfirming Questions** -- "What problems does it have?" (89% disclosure) vs. "It doesn't have any problems, does it?" (61%) vs. "What can you tell me about it?" (8%).
- **Inside View vs. Outside View** -- inside view is your optimistic self-assessment. Outside view is the base rates. Trust the outside view. Kahneman's curriculum group predicted 1.5-2.5 years; base rates said 7-10 years; it took 8.
- **Base Rates** -- aggregate data on how situations like yours turn out. Trust them like hotel reviews. Ask experts for frequencies, not predictions.
- **Zoom Out, Zoom In** -- outside view (base rates) + close-up investigation (texture that numbers miss). Both together.
- **Ooch** -- construct small experiments to test hypotheses before committing. CarsDirect: built a fake website, sold 3 cars overnight, founded the company. But don't ooch when you already know what's right and are just delaying.

**Product applications:**

| Context | Application | Example |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| **Team disagreement** | Ask "What would have to be true?" | Transforms argument into collaborative assumption-testing |
| **Evaluating a candidate** | Use disconfirming questions | "Who were the last three to leave? What are they doing now?" |
| **Market assessment** | Zoom out (base rates) + zoom in (real users) | Industry failure rate + direct customer observation |
| **Business plan confidence** | Apply outside view | "What percentage of businesses like this succeed?" |
| **New feature decision** | Ooch before committing | Build a fake door test or concierge version before full development |
| **Expert consultation** | Ask for base rates, not predictions | "What percentage of cases like this succeed?" not "Do you think I'll win?" |

**Copy patterns:**
- "What would have to be true for this to be the best choice?"
- "What problems does this have?"
- "What do the base rates say?"
- "Who has already tried this, and what happened?"
- "Let's test it before we commit."
- "What's the most likely way I could fail to get the right information here?"

**Ethical boundary:** Deliberate mistakes (Schoemaker) -- intentionally testing assumptions you've never questioned -- are a legitimate reality-testing tool. But overt deception of customers or stakeholders to "test" their reactions crosses the line. Reality-test with honest probing, not manipulation.

See: [references/reality-test.md](references/reality-test.md) for the inside/outside view framework, disconfirming question templates, ooching methodology, and base-rate collection exercises.

### 3. Attain Distance Before Deciding (A)

**Core concept:** Short-term emotion is not the enemy -- visceral feelings contain real information. The problem is when they become the only voice at the table, crowding out long-term values. Loss aversion (2-4x stronger than equivalent gains), mere exposure (familiarity breeds preference), and status-quo bias conspire to make the present feel more important than the future.

**Why it works:** Study: 66% of students chose Job B for themselves; 83% recommended Job B for their best friend. Distance yields clarity because it naturally prioritizes the most important factor and downplays short-term emotion. Andy Grove's "What would our successors do?" broke Intel's paralysis about exiting memory chips -- the outsider perspective strips historical and political weight.

**Key insights:**
- **10/10/10** (Suzy Welch) -- "How will I feel about this 10 minutes from now? 10 months? 10 years?" Levels the emotional playing field between present intensity and future fuzziness.
- **"What would I tell my best friend to do?"** -- the single most effective question for personal decisions. We see the forest for friends; we get stuck in the trees for ourselves.
- **"What would my successor do?"** -- strips emotional attachment to history. Grove to Moore: "If the board brought in a new CEO, what would he do?" Moore: "Get us out of memories." Grove: "Why shouldn't we do it ourselves?"
- **Core Priorities** -- when distancing doesn't resolve a decision, it signals a conflict among long-term values. Name the conflict explicitly.
- **Stop-Doing List** (Jim Collins) -- urgencies always crowd out priorities. MIT study: managers' weekly activities had zero connection to their stated core priorities.
- **Mere Exposure Principle** -- repetition breeds comfort. "Institutional truths" may only feel true because they've been repeated, not proven. Three exposures to a false statement increased its rated truthfulness.

**Product applications:**

| Context | Application | Example |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| **Personal career decision** | Run 10/10/10 | "10 minutes: terrified. 10 months: proud. 10 years: won't matter." |
| **Organizational inertia** | Ask "What would our successors do?" | Strips the weight of sunk costs and political history |
| **Competing priorities** | Name core values explicitly | "Is the customer the volunteer surgeon or the patient?" settled years of board conflict |
| **Time allocation** | Build a stop-doing list | What would you stop doing if you had 10 years to live but $20M? |
| **Status quo trap** | Identify mere exposure bias | "Do we believe this because it's true or because we've said it 100 times?" |
| **Hourly focus** | Bregman's hourly beep | Timer every hour: "Am I doing what I most need to be doing right now?" |

**Copy patterns:**
- "How will I feel about this in 10 months?"
- "What would I tell my best friend to do?"
- "If we got kicked out and the board brought in someone new, what would they do?"
- "What would I stop doing?"
- "Do we believe this because it's true or because we've repeated it?"

**Ethical boundary:** Distancing techniques are for gaining clarity, not for suppressing legitimate emotion. If every analysis says "proceed" but your gut screams "wrong," the gut may be detecting a pattern your conscious mind can't articulate. A "leemer" (pilot term for a vague sense something isn't right) deserves investigation, not dismissal.

See: [references/attain-distance.md](references/attain-distance.md) for the 10/10/10 framework, core priorities identification, stop-doing list methodology, and status-quo bias exercises.

### 4. Prepare to Be Wrong (P)

**Core concept:** The future is a range, not a point. Overconfidence makes us treat it as a point. When experts are "completely certain," they're wrong 40% of the time. Counteract by bookending possibilities (lower and upper), running premortems and preparades, setting tripwires to force reconsideration, and building safety factors.

**Why it works:** Soll/Klayman: when participants considered bookends explicitly, their prediction range accuracy improved from 45% to 96% of optimal. Prospective hindsight ("It happened. Why?") generates 25% more reasons than speculation ("Could it happen?"). LPCH rapid-response tripwires: code calls dropped 71%, hospital mortality dropped 18%, 33 lives saved in 18 months.

**Key insights:**
- **Bookending** -- estimate a lower bookend (dire scenario) and upper bookend (rosy scenario) instead of a single prediction. Invest when price is near the lower bookend with large upside.
- **Premortem** (Gary Klein) -- "It's 12 months from now. The project failed. What killed it?" Everyone writes reasons independently, then shares one at a time. Surfaces threats optimism suppresses.
- **Preparade** -- the upside premortem. "It succeeded wildly. Are we ready?" Softsoap locked up the world's entire pump supply before their national launch.
- **Safety Factor** -- compute the requirement, then multiply by a buffer. Elevator cables: 11x. Software schedules: +30-50%.
- **Tripwires** -- pre-defined signals that snap you out of autopilot. Types: dates/deadlines (without them, completion drops from 66% to 25%), budgets/thresholds, partitions (individually wrapped cookies last 24 days vs. 6 days unwrapped), and pattern recognition ("Call if you are worried about a patient").
- **Realistic Job Preview** -- expose people to the warts before they start. Meta-analysis of 40 studies: consistently reduces turnover through "vaccination" against shock.
- **Procedural Justice** -- people care about whether the process was fair, not just the outcome. Let people be heard, use accurate information, apply principles consistently, explain why.

**Product applications:**

| Context | Application | Example |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| **Project planning** | Run a premortem | "It's one year from now and we failed utterly. Why?" |
| **Launch preparation** | Run a preparade | "We succeeded beyond expectations. Are we ready for the demand?" |
| **Investment decision** | Use bookending | Lower and upper scenarios; invest only near the lower bookend |
| **Schedule estimation** | Apply safety factor | Add 30-50% buffer to your best estimate |
| **Ongoing projects** | Set tripwires | "If we haven't reached X by date Y, we stop and reassess" |
| **Hiring** | Use realistic job preview | Show candidates the hardest parts before they accept |

**Copy patterns:**
- "It's one year from now and we failed. What killed us?"
- "It's one year from now and we're heroes. Are we ready?"
- "What's our tripwire? When will we know to reconsider?"
- "What's the worst realistic scenario? The best?"
- "What's our safety factor?"
- "Sometimes the best way to defend a decision is to point out its flaws."

**Ethical boundary:** Tripwires are designed to enable boldness, not caution. Setting clear boundaries (budget cap, time limit, trigger condition) creates psychological permission to commit fully until that boundary is reached. Using tripwires to avoid commitment entirely defeats their purpose.

See: [references/prepare-to-be-wrong.md](references/prepare-to-be-wrong.md) for premortem/preparade facilitation guides, tripwire design templates, bookending methodology, and safety factor calibration.

## Process Beats Analysis

The book's single most important empirical finding, applicable to every decision domain.

| Factor | Impact on Decision Success |
|--------|---------------------------|
| **Process quality** (WRAP adherence) | 6x more influence than analysis quality |
| **Analysis quality** (depth of data, sophistication of models) | 1x baseline influence |

This means: a rough analysis with an excellent process outperforms a brilliant analysis with a poor process by a wide margin.

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It Fails | Fix |
|---------|-------------|-----|
| **"Whether or not" framing** | Reduces options to a binary; fails 52% of the time | Apply Vanishing Options Test; generate at least two real alternatives |
| **Seeking confirming data** | 2x bias toward confirming information | Ask "What problems does this have?"; seek the opposite |
| **Trusting expert predictions** | Experts do worse than base-rate extrapolation | Ask experts for base rates, not predictions |
| **Deciding in emotional heat** | Short-term intensity crowds out long-term values | Run 10/10/10; ask "What would my best friend say?" |
| **Single-point prediction** | When "completely certain," wrong 40% of the time | Use bookends (lower and upper scenarios) |
| **No tripwires** | Autopilot leads to escalation of commitment | Set dates, budgets, and pattern triggers before starting |
| **Sham options** | Easy consensus may mean only one real option | Test: if your team disagrees about which is best, you have real options |
| **Ooching when you should commit** | Using experiments as emotional delay tactics | If you already know, commit. Ooch only when genuinely uncertain. |

## Quick Diagnostic

| Question | If No... | Action |
|----------|----------|--------|
| Are you considering at least two genuine alternatives? | Narrow framing -- "whether or not" trap | Run Vanishing Options Test or AND Not OR |
| Have you sought disconfirming information? | Confirmation bias -- "cooking the books" | Ask "What problems does this have?" and "What would have to be true?" |
| Do you know the base rates for situations like this? | Inside view dominating -- optimistic self-assessment | Find the outside view: "What percentage of cases like this succeed?" |
| Have you run a small experiment before committing? | Predicting instead of testing | Ooch: build a cheap test of your core assumption |
| Have you applied 10/10/10 or "best friend" test? | Short-term emotion may be distorting | Run the distance test before finalizing |
| Do you have a premortem documented? | Optimism is suppressing risk awareness | "It failed. Why?" -- everyone writes independently |
| Have you set a tripwire? | No mechanism to force reconsideration | Define the date, budget, or trigger that would make you reassess |
| Would a new CEO make the same decision? | Sunk cost and politics are distorting | Ask "What would our successors do?" |
