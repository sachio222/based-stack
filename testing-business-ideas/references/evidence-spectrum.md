# The Evidence Spectrum: What Counts as Real Validation

Evidence is not binary. There is a spectrum from "someone said something nice" to "someone gave us money for something that doesn't exist yet." Teams that treat all evidence equally over-invest in bad ideas and under-invest in good ones. The Evidence Spectrum gives a precise framework for calibrating what your data actually proves.

## The Core Distinction: What People Say vs. What People Do

Human behavior is poorly predicted by self-report. People are consistently optimistic about their future behavior, especially in response to questions from someone who clearly wants a "yes." In testing contexts, the gap between stated intent and actual behavior is enormous.

| Evidence Type | Basis | Reliability | Example |
|---------------|-------|------------|---------|
| **Stated opinion** | What they say when asked | Very low | "I think I'd use that" |
| **Stated intent** | What they say they will do | Low | "I would definitely sign up for this" |
| **Low-commitment behavior** | What they do with no friction | Medium | Clicked an ad; signed up for a free waitlist |
| **Medium-commitment behavior** | What they do with moderate friction | High | Completed a multi-step form; attended a demo |
| **High-commitment behavior** | What they do when it costs something | Very high | Paid a deposit; pre-ordered; signed LOI |
| **Sustained behavior** | What they do repeatedly over time | Highest | Used the product weekly for 3 months |

## ILI: Initial Level of Interest

ILI is the first behavioral signal that a customer has any interest at all. It answers one question: "Is there any market signal here?"

**ILI experiments:** Fake door ads, landing page with email capture, explainer video views with click-through.

**What ILI proves:**
- Enough people found the concept interesting enough to take a low-cost action
- The value proposition is communicable (they understood it well enough to respond)
- There is at least a segment of the market that may want this

**What ILI does NOT prove:**
- That people will use the product
- That people will pay for it
- That people will return to it
- That the problem is important enough for long-term commitment

**ILI threshold guidance:**
- Below 2-5% conversion on a well-targeted ad: weak or no signal
- 5-15% conversion: moderate ILI; worth continuing to OLI
- Above 15%: strong ILI; high priority to advance to OLI testing

These thresholds are contextual. A niche B2B product targeting 200 companies has different expectations than a consumer product targeting millions.

## OLI: Ongoing Level of Interest

OLI is evidence that interest persists when customers face friction -- typically friction in the form of time, commitment, or money. It answers: "Is the interest real, or just initial curiosity?"

**OLI experiments:** Presales, pilot programs with fees, concierge with payment, repeated voluntary use of a prototype.

**What OLI proves:**
- Interest is strong enough to overcome a meaningful barrier
- The problem is important enough to motivate action beyond initial curiosity
- There is willingness to pay (if the OLI experiment involved money)

**The ILI-to-OLI conversion drop:** A typical ILI-to-OLI conversion funnel looks like this:
- 100 people see the ad (ILI)
- 15 click through to the landing page
- 8 sign up for the waitlist (email = low commitment ILI)
- 2 complete the detailed signup form (medium commitment ILI)
- 0.5 pre-order the product (OLI)

Each friction point eliminates the less-committed segment. The 0.5% who pre-order are more predictive of real customers than the 15% who clicked.

**OLI threshold guidance:**
- Even 1-3 paying customers in a presale constitutes meaningful OLI for a new idea
- 10+ paying customers in a presale before building is strong validation
- Sustained voluntary use for 4+ weeks is strong OLI for a free product

## Evidence Confidence Levels

The Strategyzer framework defines five confidence levels:

| Level | Description | Evidence Required |
|-------|-------------|------------------|
| **Very confident** | Multiple behavioral experiments, each confirming at scale, OLI established | Multiple presale campaigns; sustained paid usage; cohort retention above threshold |
| **Confident** | At least one OLI experiment with meaningful sample; behavioral data | Presale with 10+ orders; pilot program with fee paid; real usage data |
| **Moderately confident** | Multiple ILI experiments confirming; at least one medium-commitment behavior | Landing page + email + multi-step form completion; consistent across 2-3 experiments |
| **Not very confident** | Some ILI; mostly stated intent; small sample | A few dozen ad clicks; interview quotes; survey results |
| **Not confident** | Stated opinion only; no behavioral experiments run | Internal team belief; customer interviews saying "sounds good" |

**The promotion rule:** Confidence only increases when new behavioral evidence is collected. More internal discussion does not increase confidence. More interviews without behavioral experiments do not increase confidence.

## The Call-to-Action Threshold

The call-to-action threshold is the boundary between weak and strong evidence. It is crossed when an experiment requires a customer to take an action that has real cost -- time, personal information, or money.

**Below the threshold (weak evidence):**
- Watching a video
- Reading a description
- Saying "this sounds interesting"
- Clicking to learn more
- Responding positively in an interview

**At the threshold (medium evidence):**
- Providing an email address
- Completing a multi-step registration form
- Booking a demo call
- Requesting a sample or trial

**Above the threshold (strong evidence):**
- Pre-ordering with payment
- Signing a letter of intent
- Paying for a pilot program
- Using a product repeatedly without being prompted

Any experiment that requires crossing the call-to-action threshold is orders of magnitude more valuable than one that does not.

## Behavioral Evidence vs. Stated Intent: A Comparison Table

| Scenario | Stated Intent Version | Behavioral Evidence Version |
|----------|----------------------|------------------------------|
| Testing if customers want a new feature | "Do you want this feature?" survey | A/B test: add the feature to half the users; measure engagement |
| Testing willingness to pay | "How much would you pay for this?" | Run a presale at the target price; measure conversion |
| Testing product adoption | "Would you use this product?" | Give them access; measure if they return after day 1 |
| Testing problem severity | "Is this a big problem for you?" | Observe them working; see how many workarounds they use |
| Testing channel fit | "How did you hear about us?" survey | Run a $200 ad experiment on the channel; measure CAC |

## Evidence Expiration and Decay

Evidence has a shelf life that depends on market stability:

| Market Condition | Evidence Half-Life | Notes |
|-----------------|---------------------|-------|
| Stable, slow-moving market | 18-24 months | B2B enterprise; traditional industries |
| Normal consumer market | 12-18 months | Consumer apps; services |
| Fast-moving technology market | 6-12 months | AI, social media, consumer tech |
| Post-disruption market | 3-6 months | Markets actively being restructured by a new entrant |
| Post-major-event market | Re-validate immediately | Pandemic, regulation change, major competitor collapse |

**Stale evidence is often worse than no evidence.** Stale evidence produces false confidence. Teams that have "already validated" an assumption 3 years ago often stop questioning it -- even as the market moves.

## Evidence Calibration Checklist

Before claiming an assumption is validated:

- [ ] Is the evidence behavioral (what people did) rather than stated (what people said)?
- [ ] Did the experiment cross the call-to-action threshold?
- [ ] Was the sample drawn from actual target customers, not friends or colleagues?
- [ ] Is the evidence volume sufficient for the confidence level claimed?
- [ ] Is the evidence recent enough given the market's pace of change?
- [ ] Has the evidence been reviewed against pre-set success criteria, not post-hoc?
- [ ] Has confirmation bias been explicitly checked for?

## Adjusting Claims to Evidence Level

| If your evidence is... | You can validly claim... | You cannot yet claim... |
|------------------------|-------------------------|------------------------|
| Survey results | "There is stated interest in solving this problem" | "Customers will use our solution" |
| Landing page signups | "Enough people understood and responded to the value prop to justify a deeper test" | "There is demand for the product" |
| Free trial signups | "People are willing to try this at zero cost" | "People will pay for this" |
| One presale customer | "At least one person valued this enough to pay" | "There is a sustainable market" |
| 20 paying pilot customers | "There is a real market signal at this price" | "The product will scale" |
| Cohort with 40% week-4 retention | "A meaningful segment finds ongoing value" | "All customers will have this retention" |
