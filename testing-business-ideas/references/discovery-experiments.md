# Discovery Experiments: Exploring Desirability Before Building

Discovery experiments are the cheapest and fastest ways to find out if a problem is real and if people are interested in a solution. They are designed for exploration and initial signal generation -- ILI rather than OLI. Use discovery experiments before investing in any prototyping or production work.

The goal of a discovery experiment is not proof. It is signal. A positive signal justifies moving to validation. A negative signal saves weeks or months of wasted work.

## Experiment Library: Exploration

### Customer Interview

**Definition:** A structured conversation with a target customer to understand their problems, behaviors, and current solutions -- not to pitch or validate your idea.

**Purpose:** Generate hypotheses. Understand the problem space. Discover workarounds customers already use.

**When to use:**
- Very early, before any solution direction is formed
- When the problem definition is unclear
- When the target customer segment is not well understood

**How to run:**
- Recruit 5-15 people who match your target customer profile
- Ask about past behavior, not hypothetical future behavior ("Tell me about the last time X happened" not "Would you use Y?")
- Follow Rob Fitzpatrick's Mom Test principles: ask for stories, not opinions
- Do not pitch. Do not ask if they like your idea. Listen.

**Evidence generated:** Qualitative insight; hypothesis raw material. Not behavioral evidence.

**Time/cost:** 1-2 weeks, low cost (recruiting time + 30-60 min per interview)

**Evidence strength:** Very weak (stated). Strong for hypothesis generation.

---

### Ethnography / Job Shadowing

**Definition:** Observe target customers in their natural environment, performing the tasks your product might affect.

**Purpose:** Discover what customers actually do (behavior), not what they say they do. Uncover problems they do not know they have. Identify workarounds invisible in interviews.

**When to use:**
- When the problem involves complex workflows
- When customers are unable to articulate their own behavior
- When you suspect the stated problem is not the real problem

**How to run:**
- Request permission to observe for 2-4 hours
- Say as little as possible; observe and take notes
- Note every time someone works around a system, re-does something, or expresses frustration
- Count how often specific events occur

**Evidence generated:** Observational behavioral data. Strong for problem discovery.

**Time/cost:** 1-3 days, low to medium cost

**Evidence strength:** Weak on its own but uniquely valuable for problem validation

---

### Survey

**Definition:** A structured questionnaire sent to a target population.

**Purpose:** Measure stated preferences, awareness, and attitudes at scale. Identify patterns across large groups.

**When to use:**
- When you need scale that interviews cannot provide
- When you want to segment a population by attitudes or behaviors
- When you want to understand ranking or prioritization ("which of these problems is most important?")

**How NOT to use:**
- Do not use surveys to validate willingness to pay
- Do not use surveys to predict adoption
- Do not use survey results as evidence in investment decisions

**Evidence generated:** Stated preferences. Weak as validation, useful for hypothesis generation.

**Time/cost:** 1 week, low cost

**Evidence strength:** Very weak for validation; moderate for hypothesis generation

## Experiment Library: Data Analysis

### Search Trend Analysis

**Definition:** Use Google Trends, keyword research tools, or platform search data to analyze whether people are actively searching for a solution to the problem you are solving.

**Purpose:** Validate that demand exists at market scale before running behavioral experiments. Identify whether interest is growing, stable, or declining.

**When to use:**
- When evaluating a new market or product category
- When assessing whether a trend is real or media-driven
- Before investing in SEO or paid search

**How to run:**
- Search for the problem in Google Trends (not the solution -- the problem)
- Look for consistent search volume over 12+ months, not a single spike
- Compare relative volume against adjacent categories
- Use keyword tools to assess monthly search volume and competition

**Evidence generated:** Indirect behavioral evidence (search behavior is behavioral). Moderate strength.

**Time/cost:** Half a day, free

**Evidence strength:** Moderate; shows problem awareness, not solution demand

---

### Forum and Community Research

**Definition:** Mining Reddit, Quora, Facebook Groups, Discord servers, or industry forums for evidence that the problem is real and discussed.

**Purpose:** Find organic evidence of problem frequency, severity, and current workarounds. Identify exact language customers use to describe their problems.

**When to use:**
- Early in problem definition
- When building copy and positioning
- When assessing whether the problem is a hair-on-fire vs. a nice-to-have

**How to run:**
- Search for the problem description in relevant communities
- Look for post frequency, engagement, and upvotes as proxies for severity
- Note exact phrases and language used -- this is gold for copywriting
- Count how many posts describe the same problem without a good solution

**Evidence generated:** Indirect behavioral evidence; moderate strength for problem validation

**Time/cost:** Half to one day, free

**Evidence strength:** Moderate; organic, unbiased problem evidence

---

### Support Ticket and Review Mining

**Definition:** Analyze competitor product reviews (App Store, G2, Capterra, Amazon, Yelp) or support ticket themes to identify unmet needs and recurring frustrations.

**Purpose:** Find evidence that the problem your product solves is common, persistent, and not being addressed by incumbents.

**When to use:**
- When a competitive product exists
- When defining the differentiating problem your product will solve
- When writing positioning and copy

**Evidence generated:** Behavioral-adjacent; real customer language about real frustrations

**Time/cost:** Half to one day, free

**Evidence strength:** Moderate; shows persistent unmet need in existing market

---

## Experiment Library: Interest Discovery / Fake Doors

### Fake Door Test (Concept Ad)

**Definition:** Run an advertisement for a product or feature that does not yet exist. Measure whether people click, sign up, or express interest.

**Purpose:** Generate ILI at market scale. Answer "Is there enough interest to justify building this?" before building anything.

**When to use:**
- Before investing in any product development
- When you want to test market-level demand, not just individual interest
- When you want to compare multiple value propositions

**How to run:**
1. Write ad copy describing the product's core benefit
2. Design a simple landing page explaining the concept
3. Set up an email capture or "notify me" CTA
4. Run targeted ads to your exact target customer (Facebook, Instagram, Google, LinkedIn)
5. Budget $100-500 for the test
6. Measure click-through rate and conversion to email capture
7. When users try to access what does not exist: explain the product is not yet built and ask them to join the waitlist

**Ethical note:** Users must be informed that the product does not yet exist. Do not collect payment on a fake door.

**Evidence generated:** Behavioral ILI (clicks, signups)

**Time/cost:** 2-5 days, $100-500

**Evidence strength:** Moderate ILI; strong signal for go/no-go

---

### 404 Test (Feature Stub)

**Definition:** Add a link or button for a feature that does not exist to a live product. Track clicks. When clicked, show a "coming soon" or "we are working on this" message.

**Purpose:** Measure interest in a feature from existing users without building it.

**When to use:**
- For products with existing user bases
- When prioritizing among multiple potential features
- When you want to measure relative interest across options

**Evidence generated:** Behavioral ILI from real users in real context

**Time/cost:** 1-2 days to implement, minimal cost

**Evidence strength:** Moderate; strong relative ranking data

---

### Landing Page Test

**Definition:** Build a single page describing the product with a clear value proposition and a call-to-action (email signup, waitlist, "request access").

**Purpose:** Test the value proposition's clarity and appeal before building the product. Measure email capture as ILI.

**When to use:**
- To test a specific value proposition statement
- To compare multiple value propositions against each other (A/B)
- To build a waitlist before launch

**Evidence generated:** Behavioral ILI (email signups)

**Time/cost:** 1-3 days, low cost

**Evidence strength:** Moderate ILI; email capture is a real behavioral step

---

## Experiment Library: Discussion Prototypes

### Paper Prototype

**Definition:** A hand-drawn or roughly sketched representation of the product's interface, walked through with target customers in a moderated session.

**Purpose:** Test whether the concept is understandable and the value proposition is clear before building anything digital.

**When to use:**
- When validating a complex UX flow
- When testing multiple design concepts quickly
- Before investing in digital prototyping

**Evidence generated:** Qualitative behavioral feedback (reactions, confusion, excitement)

**Time/cost:** 1-2 days, very low cost

**Evidence strength:** Weak for demand validation; strong for usability and concept clarity

---

### Explainer Video

**Definition:** A 1-4 minute video demonstrating or explaining the product concept. May be animated, screencast, or narrated slides.

**Purpose:** Test whether the concept is communicable and generates interest at a low-touch scale.

**When to use:**
- For complex products hard to explain in text
- When you want to test messaging before building
- When you want to create sharable content for initial distribution

**Evidence generated:** Views, shares, click-throughs, email signups as ILI

**Time/cost:** 2-7 days, $0-2,000 depending on production

**Evidence strength:** Moderate ILI; viral spread provides additional signal

---

## Pretotype Types (Savoia)

### Fake Door Pretotype

The product does not exist. The "door" (ad, button, listing) is real. Measures whether people will seek out the product before it is built.

**Example:** Buffer's early landing page. Joel Gascoigne created a page describing a social media scheduler, linked to a pricing page. Clicking any plan showed "we're not ready yet" and asked for email. Enough signups justified building.

---

### Pinocchio Pretotype

A physical, non-functional mock-up of the product, used to test whether people respond to the real object. Named after the puppet that wanted to be a real boy.

**Example:** To test whether people would use a voice-activated device in the kitchen, build a fake device from cardboard and wood. Have someone respond via Bluetooth. Observe whether participants naturally interact with it.

---

### Provincial Pretotype

Test the idea in a limited, local context before scaling. Use a small geography, a single channel, or a single customer segment as a proxy for the larger market.

**Example:** Before launching nationally, run a fake door test in one city. If 12% of Denver residents who see the ad sign up, that is meaningful ILI for the national launch decision.

---

### Re-Label Pretotype

Apply your concept's label to an existing product to test whether the positioning and framing generate interest -- before building anything new.

**Example:** Take an existing generic service and rebrand it with your value proposition. Test whether the rebranding generates a different level of interest or a higher willingness to pay.

---

## Discovery Experiment Selection Guide

| Question | Recommended Experiment |
|----------|----------------------|
| Does this problem exist? | Customer interview, ethnography |
| How common is this problem? | Search trends, forum research, survey |
| Is there unmet need in the market? | Review and support ticket mining |
| Will anyone look for this solution? | Fake door ad, landing page test |
| Is our value proposition clear? | Paper prototype, explainer video |
| Which feature do existing users want most? | 404 test, survey with existing users |
| Is there enough interest to build anything? | Fake door ad, landing page |
| Does our concept feel real and desirable? | Pinocchio or Fake Door pretotype |
