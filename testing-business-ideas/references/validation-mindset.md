# The Validation Mindset: Why Most Products Fail

The world is full of smart people who built the wrong thing. Not because they lacked talent or resources, but because they never tested whether anyone wanted what they were building before they built it. The validation mindset is the cognitive shift required to treat every unproven idea as a hypothesis rather than a fact.

## The Law of Failure

Alberto Savoia formulated the Law of Failure after reviewing decades of product launch data:

**The vast majority of new products, services, and businesses will fail -- even when executed correctly.**

The failure rate for new products is 70-90%. This is not primarily a failure of execution. Teams that execute well on the wrong idea fail just as surely as teams that execute poorly. The root cause of most failures is building something the market did not want at the price the business needed to charge.

The Law of Failure has one practical implication: before investing significantly in any idea, find out if it is one of the winners or the losers. The only way to find out is to test.

| Belief | Reality |
|--------|---------|
| Good execution ensures success | Execution on the wrong idea guarantees failure |
| Customer research predicts success | Stated preferences do not predict behavior |
| My idea is different | Every founder believes this; most are wrong |
| We can course-correct after launch | By launch, most of the investment is spent |
| Investors validate the idea | Investors are wrong as often as founders |

## Thoughtland

Thoughtland is the world of opinions, arguments, and speculation that teams inhabit when they have not yet collected real evidence. In Thoughtland:

- "I think customers will love this feature"
- "My research shows there is a gap in the market"
- "Everyone I talk to says they would use this"
- "Our competitor is doing it, so there must be demand"

None of these are data. They are opinions. Thoughtland is comfortable because opinions cannot be falsified -- you can always find support for what you believe. The real world is uncomfortable because behavior produces unambiguous data that can refute your beliefs.

**Symptoms of Thoughtland:**
- Long internal debates with no data referenced
- "I know customers want this" without behavioral evidence
- Surveys cited as proof of demand
- Competitor activity used as a proxy for market demand
- Pivots driven by opinion rather than evidence

**Escape from Thoughtland:** Design an experiment. Run it. Look at what customers did, not what they said.

## Pretotyping vs. Prototyping vs. Productyping

These three terms describe different phases of testing with fundamentally different purposes:

| Term | Purpose | Question Answered | Investment |
|------|---------|-------------------|------------|
| **Pretotype** | Test initial appeal | Should we build this at all? | Hours to days |
| **Prototype** | Test feasibility and design | Can we build this, and how? | Days to weeks |
| **Productotype** (MVP) | Test product-market fit | Will customers use this repeatedly? | Weeks to months |

**Pretotyping error:** Spending 3 months building a prototype to test whether anyone wants the concept -- work that a 3-day fake door test could have answered.

**Prototype error:** Assuming a technically feasible, well-designed product will automatically find market demand. Feasibility is not desirability.

**The sequence:** Pretotype to establish initial interest → Prototype to solve feasibility and design → Productotype to establish sustained usage and willingness to pay.

## The Three Risk Types

Every business idea carries three distinct categories of risk. All three must be addressed before a business is viable, but they should be tested in order:

### 1. Desirability Risk

**Question:** Do enough customers want this?

**Why it comes first:** If customers do not want the product, feasibility and viability are irrelevant. This is the most commonly skipped test and the most common cause of failure.

**Evidence needed:** Behavioral evidence that customers will seek out, use, and return to the solution -- not just that they express interest when asked.

### 2. Feasibility Risk

**Question:** Can we actually build this?

**Why it comes second:** Only worth answering if desirability is established. Many technically challenging products are worth building if the demand is strong. Many technically simple products fail because there is no demand.

**Evidence needed:** Technical proof-of-concept, team capability assessment, infrastructure audit.

### 3. Viability Risk

**Question:** Can we make money doing this?

**Why it comes third:** Unit economics require a real customer to calculate. Without evidence on what customers will pay, how often they will buy, and what it costs to acquire them, viability analysis is speculation.

**Evidence needed:** Actual payment data, retention cohorts, real CAC from running channels.

## The Pretotyping Manifesto (Savoia)

Savoia summarized the core pretotyping principles in a manifesto. The key tenets:

**Make sure you are building the right it before you build it right.**

The single most important question is not "how do we build this?" but "should we build this?" These are different questions that require different methods.

**Innovate fast and often; fail fast and cheap.**

Speed matters because the market changes and competitors move. Cheapness matters because the goal is to fail cheaply on bad ideas so you can invest confidently in good ones.

**Use data beats opinions in every dispute.**

In any debate about what customers want, the person with behavioral data wins. If neither party has data, neither party is right -- they are both guessing.

**Your own data beats other people's data.**

Industry statistics, case studies, and competitor data all apply to different customers at different times. Your data, from your target customer, in your specific context, is always more relevant.

**Test the key assumption of the it first.**

Not all assumptions carry equal risk. The one assumption that, if wrong, kills the entire idea -- that is the one to test first.

## Data Beats Opinions

The core operational principle of the validation mindset is simple: when there is a disagreement about what customers want, the answer is to collect behavioral data, not to have more meetings.

**Before experiment:** "I think customers want real-time notifications."
**After experiment:** "We ran a fake door test. 23% of ad viewers clicked. 11% of clicks signed up. That is ILI at a level that justifies a concierge test."

The data does not need to be perfect or large. It needs to be real -- from real customers behaving in a real context.

**Data collection practices:**
- [ ] Every product assumption is written down explicitly
- [ ] Assumptions are ranked by importance and current evidence level
- [ ] Experiments are designed to collect behavioral evidence, not stated preferences
- [ ] Success criteria are written before the experiment runs
- [ ] Results are reviewed against criteria, not interpreted post-hoc
- [ ] Decisions reference specific evidence, not general impressions
- [ ] Team debates end when someone produces behavioral data

## Validation Mindset Exercises

### The "What if I'm wrong?" exercise

For any business assumption you hold with high confidence, ask: "What would it look like if I were wrong?" Write down the falsification scenario. Then ask: "Is there any data I could collect that would tell me if I'm wrong?" If yes, collect that data before proceeding.

### The Assumption Audit

Take any business plan, product roadmap, or pitch deck. Highlight every claim about what customers will do, want, or pay. For each highlighted claim, note: (1) the type of evidence behind it, and (2) whether that evidence is behavioral or stated. Rerun the exercise with behavioral evidence for every claim that lacks it.

### The "Convince me I'm wrong" meeting

Before any major product investment, assign one team member to argue the opposite position -- that customers will not want this, will not pay this, will not use this long-term. Their job is to identify the weakest assumptions and the strongest evidence against the idea. Use this to prioritize which experiments to run next.
