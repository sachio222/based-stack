# Candor Practices: Structured Systems for Honest Group Communication

Groups default to politeness. This is not a character flaw -- it is a predictable response to the social risk of criticism. When candor can threaten belonging, most people choose belonging. The solution is not to lecture people about honesty or establish a "we value candor" norm. The solution is to build structures that make candor low-risk and expected. The practices in this reference are not communication techniques -- they are environments. When you install an After-Action Review, a BrainTrust, or a Before-Action Review, you are changing the default from "politeness unless brave" to "candor unless you opt out." Structure does the work that courage cannot.

## Why Groups Default to Politeness

Three mechanisms drive the politeness default:

1. **Status preservation** -- critical feedback risks exposing the critic as wrong, petty, or aggressive. Silence is safer.
2. **Reciprocal punishment** -- in environments without explicit candor structure, criticism invites counter-criticism. The implicit rule is mutually assured protection.
3. **Ambiguity about norms** -- when it is unclear what the group expects, people default to the universal social norm: be kind.

Structure dissolves all three. When an AAR says "we will all speak," status preservation is moot. When a BrainTrust says "problems only, no solutions," reciprocal punishment has no mechanism. When the Before-Action Review is scheduled, there is no ambiguity -- candor is the expectation.

## After-Action Review (AAR)

The AAR originated in the U.S. Army and has spread to Navy SEALs, hospitals, and high-performing sports teams. It is a structured conversation run immediately after any significant activity.

### The Five Questions

| Question | What It Generates | Facilitation Note |
|----------|------------------|------------------|
| **1. What were our intended results?** | Shared reference point for evaluation | State the original goal in specific, measurable terms -- not "we wanted to do well" |
| **2. What were our actual results?** | Honest performance accounting | Encourage specifics: numbers, names, sequences -- not impressions |
| **3. What caused our results?** | Causal analysis, not blame | Separate causes from consequences; ask "what specifically" not "who" |
| **4. What will we repeat?** | Reinforcement of what worked | Equal time to this question as to what to change -- most AARs skip it |
| **5. What will we change?** | Specific, actionable improvements | Each change should name the person responsible and the next occurrence it applies to |

### Running the AAR Without Leadership

The most effective AARs run without the formal leader present -- or with the leader as a participant, not a facilitator. When the leader facilitates, group members unconsciously filter their candor through the question "what does the leader want to hear?" A peer facilitator or no facilitator produces more accurate output.

**Format tips:**
- Run within 24 hours of the activity; memory degrades and social smoothing increases with time
- No rank or title in the room: everyone's name is their name
- Designate a scribe who captures specific action items
- Close with three named action items that will be tested before the next AAR
- Time cap: 45-60 minutes for most activities; no longer

## Before-Action Review (BAR)

The BAR runs before any significant activity and builds candor by surfacing assumptions, risks, and disagreements while they are still hypothetical. Hypothetical disagreement is far easier to voice than post-hoc criticism.

### The Four Questions

| Question | What It Generates | Facilitation Note |
|----------|------------------|------------------|
| **1. What are our intended results?** | Alignment before action | If people disagree on the goal here, the activity will expose that disagreement badly -- better to surface it now |
| **2. What challenges are we likely to encounter?** | Risk surfacing | Invite pessimism explicitly: "What's the most likely thing to go wrong?" |
| **3. What have we learned from similar situations?** | Pattern transfer | Pull from AARs of past activities; if there are no past AARs, this question reveals that gap |
| **4. What will make us successful?** | Commitment to specific behaviors | Answers should be specific behaviors, not attitudes ("we'll communicate early" not "we'll be good") |

BAR and AAR are designed to be used together. BAR defines expected results; AAR evaluates actual results against them. Groups that use both develop an institutional learning cycle.

## BrainTrust (Pixar)

The BrainTrust is Pixar's primary candor engine. It is a structured review meeting in which an in-progress film is presented to a group of senior directors who offer candid critique -- with no authority to enforce any of it.

### The Format

**Who attends:** The film's director and key creative team, plus five to eight experienced Pixar directors who are not on the film.

**What happens:** The director presents the film-in-progress (often a rough cut). BrainTrust members watch and then speak freely about problems they see.

**Time:** Typically two to four hours for a full review; shorter reviews (called "sweatbox") happen more frequently.

**Frequency:** Major BrainTrusts every few months; informal reviews as needed throughout production.

### The One Rule That Makes It Work

**BrainTrust members identify problems. They never suggest solutions.**

This rule is not politeness -- it is preservation of ownership. When a critic suggests a solution, the director faces a binary choice: accept the solution (and lose creative ownership) or reject it (and appear defensive). Either way, the conversation shifts from diagnosis to prescription, and the director's creative agency is threatened.

When critics identify only problems -- "I don't understand why the character makes that choice in the third act" -- the director retains full ownership of the solution. They can hear the problem clearly, think about it freely, and solve it in their own way. The result is that BrainTrust feedback is both more honest (critics say exactly what they see) and more effective (directors actually use it).

**The implication for any review culture:** Structure your feedback sessions so that evaluators surface problems and the creator solves them. Do not let the feedback session become a solution-generation meeting.

## Red Teaming

Red teaming assigns a small group -- ideally people not invested in the current plan -- to actively try to defeat or expose the weaknesses of that plan.

### Setup and Execution

| Phase | What Happens | Key Rule |
|-------|-------------|---------|
| **Staff the team** | 2-4 people who are not the plan's authors | Do not include people who will execute the plan; they have too much invested |
| **Brief the team** | Share the full plan, goals, and assumptions | Be complete; red teamers cannot find holes they don't know exist |
| **Red team session** | Team works to surface vulnerabilities, attack assumptions, find failure modes | No defending; the goal is to be wrong |
| **Report out** | Red team presents findings to plan authors | Plan authors listen; they do not defend during the report |
| **Iterate** | Plan authors revise based on red team findings | Not all red team findings require changes, but all require a response |

**Who to staff:** People who are smart, skeptical by nature, and not personally invested in the outcome. Rotate the role -- everyone should be a red teamer at some point, which builds the habit of self-critical thinking.

## Flash Mentoring

Flash mentoring compresses traditional mentoring into a few hours of structured, focused interaction. The primary candor output of flash mentoring is rapid vulnerability-loop formation -- which produces candor as a byproduct.

### The Format

**Time:** 2-4 hours in a single session or over one day.

**Pair:** One senior member, one junior member, with a clear domain focus.

**Structure:**
1. Senior member shares their career journey, focusing on failures, pivots, and moments of uncertainty (30-45 min)
2. Junior member asks questions freely (30-45 min)
3. Both work on a shared task together (1-2 hrs)
4. Close: each person names one thing they want to do differently based on the session

**Why it works for candor:** The senior member's opening (focused on failure and uncertainty) opens a vulnerability loop immediately. The junior member enters the interaction with permission to be honest -- the senior has already modeled it. The shared task creates real stakes: both parties care about the outcome, which surfaces genuine opinions.

## Candor vs. Brutal Honesty

These are often confused and the confusion is costly. Brutal honesty is a weapon. Candor is a tool.

| Dimension | Candor | Brutal Honesty |
|-----------|--------|---------------|
| **Target** | The work, the decision, the behavior | The person |
| **Specificity** | Precise: "this scene doesn't work because..." | General: "you don't have it" |
| **Judgment** | Descriptive: what is happening | Evaluative: what you are |
| **Tone** | Curious, problem-focused | Certain, verdict-focused |
| **Safety** | Preserves belonging | Threatens belonging |
| **Result** | Honest problems surface and get solved | Honest problems go underground |

**Candor example:** "I don't understand why the character makes that choice in the third act -- it feels unmotivated to me."

**Brutal honesty example:** "The third act is a mess and it shows you don't understand your own character."

Both say the same thing about the third act. Only one produces usable input. The distinction matters because leaders who believe they are practicing candor are often practicing brutal honesty -- and wondering why people stop bringing them real problems.

## Joe Maddon's Wine-Bowl Method

Joe Maddon, manager of the Chicago Cubs when they ended their 108-year championship drought, used a specific practice to deliver hard feedback: the wine bowl.

Before a difficult conversation, Maddon would arrange a dinner -- just the player and Maddon -- over a meal and, often, a bottle of wine. The meal-first format established belonging before feedback. The relaxed setting lowered threat. The one-on-one format eliminated the audience effect that makes feedback feel like a public verdict.

**The principle:** The container shapes the content. Hard feedback delivered in a hard container (formal meeting, performance review frame, public setting) activates threat. Hard feedback delivered in a warm container (shared meal, relaxed tone, private setting) lands as information instead of judgment. The wine bowl is not a manipulation -- it is an acknowledgment that the receiver's nervous system needs to feel safe before they can hear difficult things.

**Application patterns:**
- Difficult feedback: schedule a meal or walk, not a meeting
- Group criticism: warm up with belonging cues first; never lead with the criticism
- Ongoing feedback: deliver it through the regular flow of interaction, not as a set-aside event

## When to Use Which Practice

| Situation | Best Practice | Why |
|-----------|--------------|-----|
| Post-activity learning | After-Action Review | Captures real performance data while fresh |
| Pre-activity alignment | Before-Action Review | Surfaces hidden assumptions before they cause damage |
| Creative work in progress | BrainTrust | Preserves creator ownership while surfacing real problems |
| Stress-testing a plan | Red Teaming | Creates structured adversarial thinking |
| New relationship building | Flash Mentoring | Accelerates vulnerability-loop formation |
| Difficult individual feedback | Wine Bowl method | Separates hard content from hard container |
| Any significant activity | AAR + BAR together | Creates a full learning cycle |

## Candor Practice Setup Checklist

Use before implementing any new candor practice for the first time.

### Before-Action Setup
- [ ] Purpose of the practice is clear to all participants (this is not evaluation; this is learning)
- [ ] Roles are defined (facilitator, scribe, participants) and do not include the formal leader as facilitator
- [ ] Time limit is set and communicated before the session begins
- [ ] "Problems only, no solutions" rule is stated explicitly if running BrainTrust format
- [ ] No rank in the room: everyone participates as an equal contributor

### Environment Setup
- [ ] Session is scheduled as a distinct calendar event, not tagged onto the end of another meeting
- [ ] Space is informal enough to reduce status cues (avoid boardroom format for small groups)
- [ ] Devices away or silenced for the duration
- [ ] A warm opening occurs before any critical content (belonging cues first)
- [ ] Output format is agreed upon: what will be written down, who owns the action items

### Process Safeguards
- [ ] The first question asks for the goal before evaluating performance
- [ ] Equal time is given to "what worked" and "what to change" (most sessions over-index on problems)
- [ ] Each action item has a named owner and a specific next-occurrence date
- [ ] Performance review language is excluded from development-focused sessions
- [ ] A closing acknowledgment rounds out the session (what did each person learn or appreciate?)

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: The AAR Dry Run

Run an After-Action Review on an activity that already happened -- something low-stakes from the last week. Use all five questions. Time each question (aim for 5-8 minutes per question). Notice: which question took longest? Which produced the most hedged answers? The longest question reveals where the group has the most confusion. The most hedged answers reveal where safety is lowest. Use the output not to fix the past activity but to calibrate how you run the next real AAR.

### Exercise 2: The BrainTrust Simulation

Choose an in-progress project and present it to three to five people who are not working on it. Give them one rule: they may only name problems, not suggest solutions. After 30 minutes of feedback, send the evaluators out of the room. Sit with the project team and ask: which problems rang true? What did you already know but hadn't said aloud? The answers to the second question are the most important -- they are the things your team's internal politeness was suppressing. Run this once to see how much candor your current structure is leaving in the room.

### Exercise 3: The Candor Calibration

Collect feedback from your last three team activities. For each piece of feedback you received, classify it: Was it candor (specific, behavioral, problem-focused) or brutal honesty (general, personal, evaluative) or something in between? If more than 20% of your feedback is brutal honesty or vague pleasantry, you have a candor gap -- either the feedback is too harsh to be useful or too soft to be honest. Use this calibration to adjust both the structure (add formal practices) and the modeling (leader models candor explicitly before asking for it).
