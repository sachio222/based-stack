# Culture Audit: Diagnosing Group Health Across All Three Skills

A culture audit is an observation-based diagnostic, not a survey. Surveys measure what people say they believe. The audit measures what you can see: who talks, how long, who gets acknowledged, what happens when someone makes a mistake, whether the leader asks questions or delivers pronouncements. This reference provides a complete observational framework organized by all three Culture Code skills -- Safety, Vulnerability, and Purpose -- plus scoring language, red flag tables, and an action planning structure. Use it to assess a culture you are designing, evaluating, or trying to improve.

## The Three-Skill Audit Framework

| Skill | Core Question | What You're Looking For |
|-------|---------------|------------------------|
| **Safety** | Are people free from threat here? | Belonging cues density, bad apple tolerance, threshold moment quality, voice distribution |
| **Vulnerability** | Do people take real interpersonal risks here? | Leader vulnerability frequency, candor practice existence, feedback separation, team autonomy |
| **Purpose** | Do people know where they're going and why it matters? | Priority clarity, catchphrase use, proficiency/creativity alignment, artifact presence, measurement alignment |

The three skills are sequential and dependent. Safety is the precondition for vulnerability. Vulnerability is the precondition for purpose. You can layer on purpose signals in a low-safety environment, but they will read as propaganda, not motivation. Fix the foundation before adding the floors.

### Bad Apple Tolerance

| Indicator | Observation |
|-----------|------------|
| How long has the most corrosive behavior pattern been present? | |
| Has leadership named the behavior pattern explicitly? | |
| Has leadership addressed it with the individual? | |
| Has the group's performance visibly dropped in response to this behavior? | |
| Is politeness covering for a real problem that everyone sees? | |

**Threshold:** Any tolerated bad apple behavior that has been present for more than 30 days without direct address is an active safety threat. Research shows this alone produces a 30-40% performance reduction.

## Vulnerability Audit

### Leader Vulnerability Frequency

| Behavior | Frequency in Last 30 Days |
|----------|--------------------------|
| Leader named a specific mistake or failure in a group setting | |
| Leader asked for feedback using a specific question | |
| Leader said "I don't know" or "I might be wrong" | |
| Leader credited a team member for solving a problem the leader could not | |
| Leader visibly received critical feedback without defensiveness | |

**Threshold:** Zero occurrences of any of these in 30 days indicates a vulnerability-closed environment. The leader is either unaware or actively resistant to the vulnerability pattern.

**Threshold:** Groups with no structured candor practice produce only accidental candor. Politeness is the reliable default; honesty is the occasional exception.

## Purpose Audit

### Priority Clarity

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| Can you name the top three priorities without looking? | |
| Can the average team member name them without looking? | |
| Are the priorities ranked (not just listed)? | |
| Are the priorities repeated in regular communication (not just in the handbook)? | |
| When priorities conflict, is the resolution clear from the ranking? | |

**Benchmark:** Executives predict that 64% of employees can name the top three priorities. The actual number is typically 2%. Assume the gap exists until disproven.

## Scoring Guide: Observable Behaviors by Level

### Safety

| Score | What You Can Observe |
|-------|---------------------|
| **1-3** | New members receive generic welcome; leader rarely uses names; voice distribution is highly unequal; bad apple behaviors tolerated indefinitely; space design creates isolation (cubicles, barriers, separate break schedules) |
| **4-6** | Some belonging cues present but inconsistent; threshold moments vary by who happens to be available; voice distribution unequal but not extreme; bad apple behavior addressed only when it escalates; some eye contact and engagement from leader |
| **7-9** | Consistent belonging cues from leader and senior members; threshold moments are designed and executed reliably; voice distribution roughly equal in meetings; bad apple behaviors addressed promptly with behavioral specificity; space design promotes unplanned encounters |
| **10** | All of the above plus: belonging cues happen peer-to-peer without leader prompting; threshold moment is a defined protocol; physical space has been explicitly designed for collision density; bad apples are identified and addressed within days, not weeks |

### Vulnerability

| Score | What You Can Observe |
|-------|---------------------|
| **1-3** | Leader never names mistakes; no structured candor practices; performance and development are combined; team waits for leader to make all decisions; feedback is either absent or harsh |
| **4-6** | Leader occasionally acknowledges mistakes in low-stakes situations; some informal feedback exists; development and performance conversations are nominally separate but in practice blended; team shows limited initiative |
| **7-9** | Leader regularly names specific failures and asks for feedback; at least one structured candor practice runs consistently; performance and development conversations are clearly separated; team self-organizes when leader steps back |
| **10** | All of the above plus: vulnerability norms are peer-to-peer (not just top-down); new members experience modeled vulnerability in their first week; the group has shared language for receiving vulnerability (not just for expressing it) |

### Purpose

| Score | What You Can Observe |
|-------|---------------------|
| **1-3** | Cannot name priorities; no catchphrases exist; no artifacts visible; measurement is misaligned with stated values; proficiency and creativity domains handled identically |
| **4-6** | Priorities named but not ranked; some catchphrases exist but not heard in daily interaction; some artifacts present but not referenced; measurement loosely aligned |
| **7-9** | Priorities named, ranked, and repeated; catchphrases heard in daily interactions; artifacts visible and referenced; measurement directly tied to behaviors the culture claims to value |
| **10** | All of the above plus: priorities are used to resolve real conflicts in real time; catchphrases have become part of how members describe themselves; artifacts are maintained by members, not just leaders |

## Before/After: Low-Culture vs. High-Culture Observable Behaviors

| Situation | Low-Culture Response | High-Culture Response |
|-----------|---------------------|----------------------|
| New member arrives | Handed a packet; told where to sit | Personal greeting by name; walkthrough by senior member; belonging declaration |
| Leader makes a mistake | Silence or minimizing | Leader names it specifically and publicly; asks what could have been done better |
| Group member contradicts leader | Tension; quick move to next topic | "That's interesting -- why don't you agree? I might be wrong." |
| Performance dips after a bad game | Public criticism or silent disappointment | Structured AAR within 24 hours; equal time on what worked and what to change |
| Bad apple behavior emerges | Tolerated; worked around; discussed in private | Named explicitly to the individual; addressed with specific behavioral change request |
| Priorities conflict | Decision made by whoever has most power | Decision made by referencing explicit priority ranking |
| New idea surfaces | Evaluated immediately by leader | Protected from evaluation in early stage; moved to a BrainTrust format when ready |
| Member asks for help | Perceived as weakness; handled alone | Leader models asking for help; member receives response that opens a vulnerability loop |

## Red Flags by Skill

### Safety Red Flags

| Red Flag | What It Indicates |
|----------|------------------|
| Leader habitually checks phone during conversations | Belonging cue deficit at the top |
| Same 2-3 voices dominate every meeting | Voice distribution failure |
| New member retention is low in the first 90 days | Threshold moment failure |
| "We've always had that tension" about a specific person | Bad apple tolerated |
| People say "fine" when asked how things are going | Safety too low for honest response |

### Vulnerability Red Flags

| Red Flag | What It Indicates |
|----------|------------------|
| Leader always has the answer | Vulnerability-closed environment at the top |
| Post-activity discussions are celebrations, not learning events | No AAR or equivalent; candor is not expected |
| Development feedback is delivered in performance review meetings | Feedback separation failure |
| Problems surface to leadership after they've become crises | Candor norms absent; people hide problems |
| "We don't need to do a review -- we all know what happened" | AAR avoidance; politeness over learning |

### Purpose Red Flags

| Red Flag | What It Indicates |
|----------|------------------|
| "What are our priorities?" produces different answers from different people | Priority communication failure |
| Values are on the wall but not in daily language | Decorative purpose; no behavioral heuristics |
| Creative work is evaluated on the same metrics as execution work | Proficiency/creativity misalignment |
| The measurement system rewards behaviors the leader says it doesn't value | Measurement misalignment |
| Members cannot name a recent example of a value being enacted | Purpose is verbal, not lived |

## The Complete 30-Item Culture Diagnostic Checklist

Run this checklist quarterly or when evaluating a culture change initiative.

### Safety (Items 1-10)
- [ ] New members receive a personalized welcome that uses their name
- [ ] New members hear an explicit belonging declaration in their first week
- [ ] Voice distribution in meetings is roughly equal (no one over 40%, no one under 5%)
- [ ] Leader uses individual names consistently in all interactions
- [ ] Leader signals future orientation ("when you reach X," "next time") in regular interactions
- [ ] Physical space is designed for unplanned encounters (common spaces, aligned schedules)
- [ ] Bad apple behaviors are addressed within 30 days of first appearance
- [ ] Behavioral standards for belonging are written and shared, not assumed
- [ ] First-day experience is a designed protocol, not left to whoever is available
- [ ] Belonging cues happen peer-to-peer, not only from leaders

### Vulnerability (Items 11-20)
- [ ] Leader publicly named a specific mistake or failure in the last two weeks
- [ ] Leader asked for feedback using specific, one-thing-only questions in the last month
- [ ] At least one structured candor practice (AAR, BrainTrust, BAR) is running consistently
- [ ] Performance review and development conversations are calendared as separate events
- [ ] The team self-organizes effectively when the leader steps back
- [ ] Problems surface to leadership before they become crises
- [ ] New members experience modeled vulnerability from a senior member in their first week
- [ ] The group has language for receiving vulnerability (not just for expressing it)
- [ ] First disagreements in new-group settings are welcomed explicitly by the leader
- [ ] Flash mentoring or equivalent has occurred in the last quarter

### Purpose (Items 21-30)
- [ ] Top three priorities are named, ranked, and can be recited by members without looking
- [ ] At least three action-anchored catchphrases exist and are heard in daily interaction
- [ ] Each major activity domain is classified as proficiency or creativity with corresponding leadership
- [ ] Physical artifacts are present and maintained that embody group identity and values
- [ ] Measurement is aligned with the behaviors the culture claims to value
- [ ] Priorities are used to resolve real conflicts in real time (not just stated in documents)
- [ ] Values have been revisited in the last year (Credo Challenge equivalent)
- [ ] Bar-setting behaviors from leaders are visible and physical, not just verbal
- [ ] Members can name a recent example of a stated value being enacted in a real situation
- [ ] Purpose signals reinforce Safety and Vulnerability norms (not contradict them)

## Exercises

### Exercise 1: The Fly-on-the-Wall Audit

Observe a team meeting, practice session, or group interaction as a non-participant. Take only observational notes -- no evaluation, no interpretation in the moment. After, map your notes to the three-skill framework. Where did belonging cues fire? Where were they absent? Who was vulnerable? Who deflected vulnerability? What purpose signals were present? The gap between what is visible and what the leader believes is happening is your most valuable diagnostic data.

### Exercise 2: The Priority Clarity Test

Without announcement, ask five team members individually: "What are our top three priorities, in order?" Do not prompt or coach. Record exact answers. If you get five different answers -- or if answers are ranked differently -- you have a purpose communication failure. Show the aggregated results to the leader without attribution. The results almost always surprise them. Use the surprise as the opening for a priority clarity conversation.

### Exercise 3: The Belonging Temperature Check

At the end of your next team interaction, ask each person -- in private, not in the group -- one question: "On a scale of one to ten, how much did you feel like you belonged in that interaction?" Do not explain the scale; let them interpret it. Collect the numbers. If the average is below 7, or if the range is wide (some 9s and some 4s in the same interaction), you have a belonging inequality problem. The people scoring low are already in a different psychological environment from the people scoring high. Use the scores to target your belonging cue investment.
